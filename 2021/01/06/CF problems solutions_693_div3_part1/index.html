CF problems solutions_693_div3_part1

Author: Ivan Li Date: January 6, 2021  22:28:48

This is a new series I’m opening that will be updated regularly – basically solutions to CF contest problems.

I’m not going to do the super hard questions, as no matter if I ace it or not, I don’t find it really effective. I’d rather do two medium problems instead XD

problems can be seen here -> https://codeforces.com/contest/1472

A. Cards for Friends – super easy

basically if w = $2^a$, where b is odd and h = $2^c$*d, where d is odd

then the $n_{max}$ = $2^a$ * $2^c$

B. Fair Division – super easy

  1. intuitively, if the sum of all is odd, the answer is definitely “NOOOOO!”

  2. if the sum is even, and half of the sum is also even, it is always “YES”

    rationale: no matter it’s odd or even, it can always add up to even, because 1+1=2, and 2 is even itself

    if there’s only one 1, then the sum is odd, which is case #1

  3. if the sum is even, and half of the sum is odd, it depends:

    1. if there is a 1: then there are two 1, otherwise the sum is odd -> “YES”
    2. no 1 -> “NO”

C. Long Jumps – don’t be intimidated! it’s easier than the first two!

brute force: go through every point and calculate the score O(n^2)

key find: when I arrive at point x, my later process and the total increment of score is fixed

so we just need to run the loop reversely, taking advantage of the already calculated indexes, and accelerate the algorithm to strictly O(n)

D. Even-Odd Game – Not that straightforward!

An intuitive solution might be:

Alice and Bob chooses even and odd values from the greatest to the smallest respectively.
If Alice or Bob runs out of numbers of their desire, they try to take away the greatest number the other people can get.
End of discussion.

⬆️ is very wrong!!!

e.g. [3, 2, 1]. Alice loses with the solution above. However, if she chooses 3, and Bob is forced to choose 2, leaving the two players both scoring 0. It’s a TIE!

From this, we have to reconsider the problem: what is the optimal strategy?

To more vividly see the competition, let’s combine the two parties into a same game: Alice adds her scores to the result, while Bob lowers the result by his score. Therefore, our goal is to maximize the result.

We now successfully transfer the problem into a single-party decision problem.

Let’s suppose it’s Alice’s turn right now:

if the current maximum is even, Mary happily takes it

if the current maximum is odd, Mary still has to take it, or Bob will take it, lowering the result by more than what Mary can earn!
